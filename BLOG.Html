Gukesh Dommaraju: The Youngest World Chess Champion

New

12/12/2024

In December 2024, the chess world witnessed history as India's 18-year-old prodigy, Gukesh Dommaraju, became the youngest-ever World Chess Champion. He defeated reigning champion Ding Liren of China in a tense 14-game match in Singapore.

The Championship Match

The final match between Gukesh and Ding was an intense battle of skill and endurance. Nine out of the 14 games ended in draws, showcasing the high level of play from both competitors. The turning point came in the final game when Ding, under time pressure, made a costly mistake. Gukesh capitalized on the opportunity, securing a 7.5-6.5 victory and taking home the title.

Gukesh's Winning Strategy

Throughout the tournament, Gukesh displayed remarkable composure and preparation. His opening choices were solid, and his middle-game calculations were precise. He surprised Ding with aggressive play in crucial moments, keeping the defending champion on edge.

The Winning Moment

The final game saw Gukesh in a slightly better position heading into the endgame. As the clock ticked down, Ding made an inaccurate bishop move, giving Gukesh an opening. With flawless technique, Gukesh converted his advantage into a historic win. As soon as Ding resigned, the young Indian grandmaster put his hands over his face, overwhelmed with emotion.

Reactions and Interviews

In the post-match interview, Gukesh said, "This is a dream come true. I have worked so hard for this moment, and to achieve it at such a young age is unbelievable."

Former world champion Viswanathan Anand praised Gukesh’s performance, calling it a "monumental achievement" and stating that Indian chess had entered a new era. Chess fans and experts worldwide hailed Gukesh as a future legend in the making.

A New Era for Indian Chess

With this victory, Gukesh becomes only the second Indian to win the World Chess Championship, following in the footsteps of Anand. His triumph has ignited a new wave of enthusiasm for chess in India, inspiring young players to pursue the game seriously.

Conclusion

Gukesh Dommaraju's journey from a young chess enthusiast to the youngest World Chess Champion is a testament to his dedication and passion. His victory marks a new chapter in chess history and solidifies India's dominance in the sport.

Vincent Keymer, who helped Gukesh win world title, clinches Weissenhaus leg of Freestyle Chess Tour 2025

16/02/2025

In February 2025, the world of chess witnessed a remarkable event as Germany's young talent, Vincent Keymer, emerged victorious in the Freestyle Chess G.O.A.T. Challenge. This tournament, held at Gut Weißenhaus in Wangels, Germany, brought together some of the world's top chess grandmasters to compete in a unique format known as Freestyle Chess.

What is Freestyle Chess?

Freestyle Chess, inspired by Bobby Fischer's Chess960, introduces random starting positions for the pieces. This variation eliminates the possibility of extensive pre-game preparation and emphasizes creativity and adaptability, as players cannot rely on traditional opening strategies.

The Tournament Setup

The Freestyle Chess G.O.A.T. Challenge took place from February 9 to 16, 2024. Eight elite grandmasters participated, including former World Champion Magnus Carlsen, reigning World Champion Ding Liren, and Germany's own Vincent Keymer. The tournament featured a prize fund of $200,000, with $60,000 awarded to the winner.

Vincent Keymer's Journey

Vincent Keymer, born on November 15, 2004, in Mainz, Germany, has been recognized as one of the country's most promising chess talents. He achieved the title of Grandmaster in 2020 and has since been climbing the ranks in international chess.

The Road to Victory

In the preliminary rapid games, Keymer showcased his skill by securing second place, setting the stage for the knockout rounds. In the semifinals, he faced the formidable Magnus Carlsen. Demonstrating exceptional strategy and composure, Keymer defeated Carlsen, advancing to the finals.

The Championship Match

In the final match, Keymer faced American grandmaster Fabiano Caruana. The games were intense, with both players displaying high-level tactics and creativity. Keymer managed to secure a 1.5-0.5 victory, clinching the championship title.

Reactions and Interviews

Following his victory, Keymer expressed his elation, stating, "Winning this tournament is a dream come true. Competing against the world's best and coming out on top is an incredible feeling." Magnus Carlsen praised Keymer's performance, noting his adaptability and innovative approach to the game.

Impact on the Chess World

Keymer's triumph in the Freestyle Chess G.O.A.T. Challenge has garnered significant attention in the chess community. His success not only highlights his individual talent but also brings focus to the growing popularity of chess variants like Freestyle Chess. This victory is expected to inspire a new generation of players to explore and embrace diverse forms of the game.

Conclusion

Vincent Keymer's victory in the 2024 Freestyle Chess G.O.A.T. Challenge marks a significant milestone in his career and the evolution of chess. His ability to adapt and excel in this innovative format showcases the dynamic nature of the game and sets the stage for future developments in the world of chess.

Praggnanandhaa Wins Tata Steel Chess 2025 After Dramatic Tiebreak Against Gukesh

02/02/2025

WIJK AAN ZEE, Netherlands – In a tournament filled with surprises, comebacks, and nail-biting finishes, 18-year-old R Praggnanandhaa emerged as the Tata Steel Chess 2025 champion after a dramatic tiebreak showdown against fellow Indian Grandmaster Gukesh Dommaraju. The victory cemented Praggnanandhaa’s place among the world’s elite as he lifted one of the most prestigious trophies in chess.

A Tournament of Twists and Turns

The tournament saw intense battles right from the start. Gukesh took an early lead, scoring critical wins against Levon Aronian and Alireza Firouzja. His precise positional play and fearless approach made him look like the clear favorite for the title.

Praggnanandhaa, on the other hand, had a steady but slower start. He suffered a tough loss to Anish Giri in Round 4 but bounced back with a spectacular victory against Nodirbek Abdusattorov. His ability to handle pressure and deliver strong endgames kept him in the race.

By Round 10, Gukesh was still leading, but a dramatic game against Magnus Carlsen nearly derailed his campaign. The world champion created complications, but Gukesh held his ground and forced a draw after nearly six hours of play. Meanwhile, Praggnanandhaa kept climbing, defeating Maxime Vachier-Lagrave in a stunning tactical battle.

The Final Round: A Twist in the Tale

Heading into the final round, Gukesh was half a point ahead of Praggnanandhaa. All he needed was a win to secure the title. Paired against fellow Indian GM Arjun Erigaisi, Gukesh played aggressively but misjudged a critical queen exchange. Erigaisi took control, launching a decisive attack that ended Gukesh’s game in a crushing defeat.

At the same time, Praggnanandhaa, knowing he had a chance to catch up, played a flawless game against Anish Giri. He maintained strong positional control throughout and converted his slight edge into a victory, tying with Gukesh at the top of the standings with 8.5 points each.

Tiebreak Showdown: A Clash of India’s Finest

With both players tied, the tournament rules required a two-game rapid tiebreak to determine the champion. The tension in the hall was palpable as the two young Indian grandmasters prepared for one final battle.

In the first game, Praggnanandhaa, playing with White, opted for a solid yet dynamic approach. He built up pressure on the queenside, forcing Gukesh into a passive position. With precise calculation, he won a pawn and transitioned into an endgame where his passed pawn proved decisive. Gukesh fought hard but had to resign after 52 moves.

The second game was Gukesh’s last chance to force a deciding Armageddon game. Needing a win, he launched an aggressive Sicilian Defense, trying to create chaos on the board. The game became incredibly sharp, with both players down to seconds on the clock. At one point, Gukesh sacrificed a piece for an attack, but Praggnanandhaa defended accurately, neutralizing the threats. With his last few moves, Praggnanandhaa exchanged queens, simplifying into a drawn position. Gukesh offered his hand in resignation. It was over.

The Winning Moment and Reactions

As soon as the game ended, Praggnanandhaa exhaled deeply, smiling as he leaned back in his chair. Gukesh, despite his disappointment, gave his friend a warm handshake. The crowd erupted in applause as Praggnanandhaa was crowned the Tata Steel Chess 2025 champion.

Speaking after the match, Praggnanandhaa said, "I still can't believe it. This tournament was tough from the beginning, and the tiebreak was incredibly stressful. Gukesh is a phenomenal player, and I knew it would be a very close fight."

Gukesh, though visibly disappointed, was gracious in defeat. "I gave it my all, but Prag played better today. These battles make us stronger, and I’ll be back next year," he said with a smile.

Former world champion Viswanathan Anand, who has mentored both players, watched with pride. "What we saw today was the future of Indian chess. Both of them are already among the best in the world, and this is just the beginning."

As the cameras flashed and the trophy was handed over, Praggnanandhaa lifted it high, marking yet another historic moment in Indian chess. The tournament had delivered everything—thrills, heartbreak, and a champion who proved his nerves under fire.

What Makes a Great Beginner Chess Book?

Before we jump into the list, let's talk about what actually works for new players. Because here's the thing—I've seen too many people buy "My System" by Nimzowitsch as their first chess book (it's a classic!) only to give up on page 12 when they hit the section on "overprotection of the center." Yikes.

A truly good beginner chess book should:

  • Start with the actual basics (yes, even how pieces move if needed)
  • Use lots of diagrams—because nobody wants to visualize "Nf3" in their head when they're just starting
  • Include exercises and puzzles to practice, not just endless theory
  • Explain concepts in plain English, not ancient chess notation hieroglyphics
  • Build confidence instead of making you feel dumb

Whether you're looking for chess books for adult beginners who want sophisticated explanations, or kids chess books for beginners with colorful illustrations and shorter attention spans in mind, this list has you covered.

The Top 10 Chess Books for Beginners (Ranked)

1. "Bobby Fischer Teaches Chess" – Bobby Fischer

Look, I know what you're thinking: "Isn't Bobby Fischer that controversial guy?" Yes. But here's the deal—this book is still the single best first chess book for learning tactics, hands down.

Published in 1966 (yes, it's ancient), this workbook-style gem is basically chess training disguised as a puzzle book. It focuses almost entirely on checkmate patterns and basic tactics using a programmed learning approach. You solve a puzzle, turn the page, check your answer, and move on.

Why it works: Minimal notation, lots of diagrams, gentle progression.

Best for: Absolute beginners who just learned the rules.

2. "Logical Chess: Move by Move" – Irving Chernev

If Fischer’s book teaches you what to look for, Chernev teaches you why. He explains every move in 30 complete games.

Why it works: You learn principles through real games instead of abstract rules.

Best for: Players who know the rules and have played 20–30 games.

3. "Everyone's First Chess Workbook" – Peter Giannatos

A modern, structured self‑study course with hundreds of graded exercises.

Why it works: Methodical, curriculum‑style approach from basics to ~1000–1200 level.

Best for: Self‑motivated learners who like workbooks.

4. "Play Winning Chess" – Yasser Seirawan

Friendly introduction to fundamental strategy and basic tactics.

Why it works: Conversational tone, clear ideas about force, time, and space.

Best for: Adult beginners and older teens.

5. "Winning Chess Strategy for Kids" – Jeff Coakley

Colourful, kid‑oriented introduction to strategic thinking.

Why it works: Real strategy taught with fun diagrams and challenges.

Best for: Kids 7–12 who already play casually.

6. "The Soviet Chess Primer" – Ilya Maizelis

Classic Soviet textbook covering rules, tactics, strategy, endgames, openings, and analysis.

Why it works: One‑volume course from beginner to strong club player.

Best for: Serious teens and adults.

7. "Let's Play Chess: A Step-by-Step Guide for New Players" – Bruce Pandolfini

Gentle introduction that assumes nothing.

Why it works: Slowly explains rules, notation, and simple principles.

Best for: Absolute beginners of any age.

8. "1001 Chess Exercises for Beginners"

Large collection of graded tactical puzzles.

Why it works: Builds pattern recognition through repetition.

Best for: Players who know the basics and want tactics practice.

9. "Silman's Complete Endgame Course" – Jeremy Silman

Endgames organized by rating band.

Why it works: Shows only the endgames you actually need at beginner level.

Best for: Players who reach endgames and feel lost.

10. "Chess Fundamentals" – José Raúl Capablanca

Classic on basic strategy, endgames, and positional play.

Why it’s still relevant: Principles are timeless.

Best for: Beginners with some tactics base who want strategy.

How to Choose the Right Chess Book for YOU

Here’s a simple decision table:

Your Situation Start With This Book
Complete beginner, any age "Bobby Fischer Teaches Chess"
Adult who wants sophisticated explanations "Play Winning Chess"
Child ages 7–12 "Winning Chess Strategy for Kids"
Structured learner who likes workbooks "Everyone's First Chess Workbook"
Played 30+ games, want to understand strategy "Logical Chess: Move by Move"
Serious about long‑term improvement "The Soviet Chess Primer"

Creating Your Chess Study Plan

For Adults (30–60 minutes per day):

  1. Weeks 1–2: "Bobby Fischer Teaches Chess".
  2. Weeks 3–6: One game per week from "Logical Chess: Move by Move" + 10 tactics puzzles daily.
  3. Weeks 7–12: Add endgame lessons from Silman.
  4. Ongoing: Play and review 2–3 rated games per week.

For Kids (15–30 minutes per day):

  1. Month 1: "Winning Chess Strategy for Kids" + puzzles.
  2. Month 2: "Everyone's First Chess Workbook" exercises.
  3. Month 3+: Mix puzzles, games, and review.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is "Bobby Fischer Teaches Chess" still good for beginners today?

Yes. It remains one of the strongest first tactics books because it builds pattern recognition through puzzles.

Is "Logical Chess: Move by Move" too advanced for a new player?

It’s ideal once you’ve played some games and know the rules; complete beginners should start with something simpler.

Which beginner chess books focus on strategy instead of openings?

"Play Winning Chess", "Logical Chess: Move by Move", and "Winning Chess Strategy for Kids" all emphasize strategic ideas.

Are there books that take me from beginner to intermediate?

"The Soviet Chess Primer" and "Everyone's First Chess Workbook" are both designed as full courses.

What are good books for kids who play casual chess?

"Winning Chess Strategy for Kids" and "Everyone's First Chess Workbook" work very well for school‑age players.

Are classics like "Chess Fundamentals" still worth reading?

Yes, but they’re best after you have a basic foundation from a modern beginner‑friendly book.

Which books include lots of exercises?

"Bobby Fischer Teaches Chess", "Everyone's First Chess Workbook", and "1001 Chess Exercises for Beginners" are heavily exercise‑based.

What modern books are best for beginners?

Giannatos’s workbook is a standout recent option, and Coakley’s book is a strong modern choice for kids.

The Bottom Line: Just Pick One and Start

Step 1: Make Sure You've Got the Right Lichess App

First things first: not all Lichess apps are created equal. There's the official Lichess mobile app (the one you want) and an older legacy version that some folks still have installed. If you're having tournament issues, there's a decent chance you're using the outdated one.

How to check your app

Android users: Head to the Google Play Store and search "Lichess." The correct app is called "Lichess • Free Online Chess" with the package ID org.lichess.mobileV2. If you see org.lichess.mobileapp, that's the old one—time for an upgrade.

iOS users: Go to the App Store and search "Lichess." Download the official one from the lichess.org team. You can also visit lichess.org/mobile and follow the link from there.

Once you've got the right app, log in (or create an account if you haven't already). You do need an account to join tournaments—anonymous play will not work.

Step 2: Finding Tournaments in the Lichess Mobile App

You're logged in. Now what? This is where things get slightly tricky because the tournament section isn't exactly front and center.

  1. Open the Lichess app homepage.
  2. Look for the menu icon (three horizontal lines) at the top-left or bottom of your screen, depending on your device.
  3. Tap it, then scroll until you find Tournaments.
  4. Tap Tournaments to open the tournaments list.

You'll see a list of upcoming tournaments, organized by start time. Arena tournaments are the most common—they're fast, continuous events where you play as many games as you can. Swiss tournaments are more structured, with set rounds and pairings.

Pro tip: If you don't see many tournaments listed, try refreshing the page or checking your time zone settings.

Step 3: Joining an Arena Tournament

You've found a tournament that looks good—maybe it's a Blitz Arena or a Rapid event. Now comes the moment of truth: joining it.

Joining an Arena tournament

  1. Tap on the tournament you want to join from the list.
  2. Review the tournament details (time control, duration, participants).
  3. Scroll down if needed and look for a green Join button.
  4. Tap Join.

The app will confirm that you're registered. If notifications are enabled, you'll get an alert when the tournament is about to start.

If there is no "Join" button

  • The tournament has started or ended: you can only join before the start time. Arenas allow late entry for a few minutes; after that, you're locked out.
  • You don't meet the requirements: some tournaments require a minimum rating, a number of games played, or team membership.
  • It's private or team-only: you may need to join a team or use a direct link (see next step).

Step 4: Joining a Private or Team Tournament from a Link

Often your coach or organizer will share a tournament link via WhatsApp, email, or Zoom chat. On mobile, these links usually open in a browser first.

Open the link in the Lichess app

  1. Tap the tournament link in WhatsApp, Zoom, etc. It will open in your browser.
  2. If you see a banner like “Open in Lichess App,” tap it.

If there is no banner, use this manual method:

  1. Copy the tournament URL from your browser’s address bar.
  2. Open the Lichess app.
  3. Use the search function and paste the URL.
  4. Open the tournament page and tap Join.

Team tournaments

For team tournaments, you must be a member of that team first.

  1. On the tournament page, tap the team name.
  2. Tap Join Team and wait for approval if required.
  3. Return to the tournament page and tap Join.

To join Educhess events, make sure you are in the Educhess Academy team.

Step 5: Understanding Tournament Requirements

Some tournaments are restricted. If you cannot join, check for requirements such as:

  • Minimum games played: for example, 20 or 50 rated games.
  • Rating limits: events may be “under 1500” or “above 1600.”
  • Team membership: team tournaments require you to be on that team.
  • Account age: some events do not allow very new accounts (for example, less than 24 hours old).

If the requirements are not met, the Join button will be missing or disabled with an explanation.

Step 6: Getting Notifications So You Don’t Miss the Start

After joining, you should make sure you receive a notification when the tournament begins.

Enable Lichess notifications on your phone

  1. Open your phone’s Settings.
  2. Go to Apps or Notifications and find Lichess.
  3. Allow notifications and alerts for the app.

Once enabled, you will get a notification a few minutes before the tournament starts. Tap it to jump straight into the tournament lobby.

Pro tip: If you miss the start of an Arena tournament, you can still join during the early minutes—go back to Tournaments and tap Join on the live event.

Arena vs Swiss: What’s the Difference?

Arena tournaments

  • Continuous play—you get a new game as soon as one ends.
  • Points based on wins, with bonuses for streaks.
  • Late join allowed for the first few minutes.
  • Great for casual and beginner‑friendly events.

Swiss tournaments

  • Fixed number of rounds and pairings.
  • Points based on wins and draws, with tiebreaks.
  • Must join before the first round starts.
  • Used for more formal or team‑based events.

For beginners and kids, Arena tournaments are usually easier and more forgiving.

Troubleshooting: Common Lichess Tournament Issues

Problem: The tournament link opens in my browser, not the app

Fix: Tap the “Open in Lichess App” banner if it appears. If not, copy the URL, open the Lichess app, and paste it into the search bar to open the tournament page.

Problem: I can’t find the “Join” button

Fix: Scroll down on the tournament details page—the button may be below the fold. If it still does not appear, check the tournament requirements (rating, games, account age, team membership).

Problem: The app crashes during tournaments

Fix: Update to the latest version of the app via the Play Store or App Store. Close other apps to free memory, then re‑open Lichess; it should reconnect you to the event automatically.

Problem: I didn’t get a notification when the tournament started

Fix: Verify that phone‑level notifications are enabled for Lichess and that battery‑saving modes are not limiting the app’s background activity.

Is It Safe for Kids to Join Lichess Tournaments?

Lichess tournaments are generally safe for kids when used correctly.

  • No default in‑game chat: players cannot freely message each other unless both accounts follow each other.
  • Kid Mode: Lichess offers a special Kid Mode that disables all communication features and locks down the account even more.
  • Moderation: Lichess moderators actively enforce fair‑play and behaviour rules.

It is still a good idea to supervise your child for the first few tournaments to help them navigate the app and manage time.
